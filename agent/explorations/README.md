## What lives here

`agent/explorations/` holds **pre-artifact strategic riffs** — structured working documents that capture a strategic direction, surface its tensions, and link to the corpus material it stands on, *before* the project commits to a final artifact form.

An exploration is what you write when you have enough conviction about a direction to want to preserve it overnight, but not enough yet to know whether it should become an exchange, a brief, a roadmap update, a new doctrine document, or something else.

## How explorations differ from other `agent/` content

| Directory | Purpose | Form |
|---|---|---|
| [`agent/exchanges/`](../exchanges/) | Multi-round formal debates with adversarial review | Numbered, indexed in [`_EXCHANGE_INDEX.md`](../exchanges/_EXCHANGE_INDEX.md), structured rounds |
| [`agent/process/`](../process/) | Audit and review protocols and their outputs | Protocol documents and dated audit artifacts |
| [`agent/doctrine/`](../doctrine/) | Settled doctrine the project commits to | Stable, citable, normative |
| `agent/explorations/` | Pre-artifact strategic riffs | Exploratory, dated, time-bounded, names its own next-step decisions |

Explorations are categorically distinct from exchanges and are **not** indexed in the exchange index. They are also distinct from the root-level [`SCRATCHPAD.md`](../../SCRATCHPAD.md), which is the steward's personal working notes; explorations are collaborative artifacts intended to be read, cited, and built on.

## What an exploration must include

Every exploration document in this directory should:

1. **Frame the riff explicitly.** Why this is being written, what is in scope, what is deliberately not.
2. **Preserve originating intent verbatim.** If the riff is downstream of a steward framing message, a practitioner conversation, or a specific source, quote it before paraphrasing.
3. **Cite the corpus.** Link to the principles, problem map, exchanges, sources, and prior artifacts the riff stands on. Use the [authoring guard](../../sources/README.md#authoring-guard--quote-actual-section-titles-from-core-normative-documents) when referencing core normative documents.
4. **Name candidate paths without picking one.** Explorations are for surfacing the option space, not for deciding among options. A riff that has already collapsed into a single path should be promoted into a brief, exchange, or doctrine document.
5. **End with explicit open questions.** What needs to be decided in the next session for the exploration to become an artifact.

## When an exploration ends

An exploration is closed when its open questions have been answered well enough that a downstream artifact (brief, exchange, doctrine, roadmap update, proposal) is the right next step. The exploration document itself stays in this directory as the historical record of how the artifact came to take its eventual form. It is not deleted or replaced.

If an exploration sits unanswered for a long time and the project's center of gravity has moved past it, the exploration should be marked closed-without-resolution in its own status block, with a short note about why the path was not pursued. This is itself useful information for future strategic riffs that may rediscover the same territory.

## Authoring conventions

- **Filename:** `<kebab-case-topic>.md` (no date prefix; the file's git history captures dates).
- **Title:** matches filename converted to title case.
- **Status block:** at the top, name the originating trigger and the open / closed state.
- **Length:** as long as the riff requires, but not longer. If a section becomes long enough to be its own artifact, consider promoting it.
- **Tone:** the same epistemic posture as the rest of the project — working claims, named uncertainty, no rhetorical flourish, no platitudes.
