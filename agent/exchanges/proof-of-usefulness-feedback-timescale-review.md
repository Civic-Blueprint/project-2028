# Proof-of-Usefulness Memo — Feedback Timescale Review

> **Status (April 2026):** Synthesized. Four rounds completed (two adversarial, two constructive). Steward synthesis and six recommendations produced. Next actions: steward decision on adoption, then practitioner critique design.
>
> **Why this exchange:** The prior exchange on housing vs. AI concluded that the strongest first public artifact was a comparative memo pairing housing permitting with AI governance. That decision solved an important problem: it answered the newcomer's likely "Why start with housing when AI is more urgent?" objection and demonstrated the framework's cross-domain method. But the current memo now makes a more demanding claim: that recursive uplift and institutional-capacity hypotheses can be pressure-tested through domains that operate at radically different speeds. The objection raised here is that this may still be too slow as a learning strategy. If housing produces real evidence only on years-long timelines, and if AI capability and concentration are moving on engineering timelines, then the project may need to distinguish between its first public explanatory artifact and its first fast-feedback empirical test. This exchange asks whether the project should reevaluate its "first mover" logic accordingly.

### Top-line challenge

Yes, this discussion is worth starting.

Not because the comparative memo was a bad idea on its own terms. It was a reasonable answer to the last question the project asked.

But your prompt surfaces a different question:

**What if the project has optimized Memo 01 for explanatory legibility when it now needs to optimize for learning velocity?**

That is a materially different standard.

If the project needs real evidence-based feedback on recursive uplift before AI-driven concentration, deployment, and governance drift outrun the framework's practical relevance, then the current proof-of-usefulness logic may be too slow. In that sense, "the game might be over" is overstated rhetorically but valid analytically as a failure mode:

**a theory that validates on years-long timelines may arrive too late to guide fast-moving domains.**

That does not mean the current memo should be discarded. It does mean the project should re-open whether one artifact is being asked to do two jobs that should now be separated.

---

### 1. The current comparative memo may solve the wrong problem

The comparative memo does several things well:

- it answers the obvious newcomer objection about AI urgency
- it demonstrates cross-domain reasoning
- it shows that the same upstream variable can produce different downstream failures at different timescales

Those are real strengths.

But none of them answers the harder question your prompt raises:

> "Can this artifact generate decision-relevant feedback fast enough to matter?"

The current memo is mostly a **diagnostic and framing artifact**. It may be useful as a public explanation of the framework's method. That is not the same thing as being a good early test of whether recursive uplift is real, or of whether the framework is identifying the right practical leverage point under current conditions.

In other words:

- the memo may be a good introduction
- it may still be a poor learning instrument

That distinction now matters more than it did in the previous exchange.

---

### 2. Housing is a weak first empirical test if feedback speed is the criterion

Housing remains a strong case for legibility, lived relevance, and visible state performance.

But if the question is now "Where can the project get meaningful evidence soon enough to revise its theory?", housing looks much weaker.

Why:

- meaningful housing outcomes often depend on multi-year implementation cycles
- even intermediate wins are easy to misread because construction, financing, infrastructure, litigation, and local politics all confound attribution
- the strongest recursive-uplift claim is not just "permits got faster" but "visible competence changed trust, coalition durability, and what became politically possible next"

That last claim is especially important. It is a sequential social claim. It requires not just better administration but observed spillover into subsequent reform capacity. That is exactly the kind of thing that usually takes time to see clearly.

So housing may still be a good **illustrative case**.

It is much less obviously a good **rapid-feedback case**.

If the project treats housing as the primary near-term empirical test of recursive uplift, it risks waiting a long time for ambiguous signals.

---

### 3. AI governance is urgent, but it is also a noisy validation domain

A natural response would be: fine, then move harder toward AI.

But this is not obviously the answer either.

AI is fast-moving, but that speed does not automatically make it a good evidence domain. Many AI-governance interventions are:

- diffuse rather than bounded
- multi-actor rather than institution-specific
- hard to evaluate causally
- vulnerable to performative compliance
- easy to confuse with broader geopolitical or market shifts

Recent public reporting does strengthen the memo's timescale argument: AI governance is increasingly being shaped through procurement, training mandates, sandboxing, technical standards, and agency guidance because legislation is too slow. That supports the diagnosis that governance mechanisms need to move faster than ordinary lawmaking.

But it does **not** solve the feedback problem.

A procurement rule, audit requirement, or model-use restriction can be adopted within months. Proving that it changed concentration dynamics, legitimacy, public capacity, or downstream power distribution is much harder.

So the adversarial conclusion is:

**AI may be the most urgent domain, while still being a poor stand-alone first domain for fast empirical validation of recursive uplift.**

That is uncomfortable, but important.

---

### 4. The project may be conflating three different "firsts"

The exchange should explicitly separate at least three questions:

1. **First public orientation memo:** What helps a serious newcomer understand the framework quickly?
2. **First empirical learning memo:** What gives the project the fastest credible feedback on whether its core hypotheses are wrong?
3. **First real-world intervention domain:** Where would the project's theory, if correct, imply action should begin?

The earlier housing-vs.-AI exchange mostly answered Question 1.

Your prompt is pushing on Question 2, and partly on Question 3.

Those are not the same question.

If the project keeps treating them as one, it may keep reaching analytically neat but strategically muddled conclusions.

The strongest immediate move is not necessarily "replace Memo 01."

It may be:

**keep the comparative memo as the public-facing orientation artifact, while separately identifying a faster-feedback domain for early empirical learning.**

That is a less dramatic revision than replacing the memo, but potentially a more important one.

---

### 5. A faster-feedback test would need different selection criteria

If the project wants real evidence-based feedback sooner, then the selection criteria for the next memo or pilot-like artifact should shift.

A fast-feedback domain should ideally have most of the following properties:

- measurable change within roughly 6 to 18 months rather than 3 to 10 years
- a bounded institutional actor rather than a global coordination problem
- public outputs that outsiders can inspect
- a plausible causal chain between intervention and observed result
- relevance to institutional capacity rather than a one-off optimization
- at least a plausible pathway from visible competence to broader trust or follow-on capacity
- enough public salience that the result matters beyond internal learning

That points away from "entire housing systems" and away from "global AI governance" as the primary validation domains.

It points more toward narrower administrative systems where performance can improve on shorter cycles and where spillover claims can at least begin to be observed.

---

### 6. The most promising search space may be narrower, faster, and more institutionally bounded

I am not claiming here that the project has already identified the right replacement domain.

But the exchange should probably look in classes of cases such as:

- permitting sub-processes with short-cycle turnaround metrics, rather than full housing supply outcomes
- benefits access or redetermination systems where administrative burden, uptake, and trust can shift relatively quickly
- public records, records access, or FOIA-style turnaround where competence and legitimacy are visibly linked
- licensing, inspection, or code-enforcement workflows where better execution could be seen and measured
- procurement or grant-disbursement processes where public-interest tooling might increase speed, auditability, and fairness

These are not recommendations yet. They are candidate search spaces.

The key point is that the project's first **evidence-generating** case may need to be:

- smaller than housing
- more bounded than AI governance
- faster than either

That may sound less grand. It may also be more scientifically useful.

---

### 7. The deepest challenge is that recursive uplift may not be a single-speed claim

There is an even harder possibility the project should not dodge.

Maybe recursive uplift has at least two layers:

- a **micro layer**, where visible administrative competence improves immediate outcomes and local trust quickly
- a **macro layer**, where that local trust actually changes coalition strength, institutional durability, and what reforms become possible next

If so, then the project should stop implying that one early memo or one early case can validate the full theory in one move.

What a faster domain may be able to test is the micro layer:

> can visibly better public execution produce near-term gains in trust, uptake, compliance, or willingness to attempt the next reform?

What it may **not** be able to test quickly is the full civilizational version of the claim.

That is not a reason to abandon the theory.

It is a reason to specify the theory more carefully so that "real evidence-based feedback" means something operational instead of aspirational.

- **Was the comparative Memo 01 a mistake?** No.
- **Is the current Memo 01 sufficient as a fast empirical test of recursive uplift?** Probably not.
- **Should the project distinguish between a public orientation artifact and a faster-feedback empirical test?** Yes.
- **Does this mean the project should immediately replace Memo 01?** Not yet.
- **Does it mean the project should reopen its first-mover logic under a new criterion centered on evidence cadence?** Yes.

### 1. Round 1 is right about the distinction, but it may overstate the failure

The current comparative memo can still do meaningful work on the first even if it is weak on the second.

- recursive uplift
- trust formation
- coalition durability
- institutional legitimacy
- what broader reforms become possible next

So speed alone is not enough.

The project does not just need a short-cycle domain. It needs a short-cycle domain whose outputs bear enough structural resemblance to the larger theory that the feedback is actually informative.

### 4. The project should separate layers of recursive uplift more explicitly

Here I think Round 1 is not just useful but probably directionally correct.

The theory is currently carrying multiple claims at once:

- that visible public competence can improve immediate outcomes
- that visible public competence can increase trust and legitimacy
- that increased trust and legitimacy can make subsequent reforms easier
- that repeated sequences of that kind can accumulate into broader recursive uplift

Those claims operate on different timescales and under different evidentiary conditions.

The project should stop talking as though one memo, one domain, or one short-cycle test can adjudicate the whole chain.

I would restate the theory in at least three layers:

1. **Execution layer:** can institutions deliver visibly better outcomes in a bounded domain?
2. **Trust layer:** does that visible performance improve trust, uptake, compliance, or willingness to engage?
3. **Sequence layer:** does that trust or legitimacy make the next reform more feasible?

That is a cleaner map than "recursive uplift" as one undifferentiated claim.

It also makes the fast-feedback question more tractable.

A bounded near-term case may be able to test parts of Layer 1 and parts of Layer 2.

It is much less likely to settle Layer 3 quickly.

That is fine. The theory gets stronger when its claims are better specified.

## Adversarial Review — Round 3 Agent (Claude, Opus)

> **Role:** Designated adversarial contributor per the [Adversarial Review Protocol](../process/adversarial-review-protocol.md), operating under Option B + Option C.
>
> **Alternative framing:** The emerging consensus from Rounds 1 and 2 is treated as a set of assertions to be tested, not as a conversation to extend. The assertions under review are: (1) `Memo 01` should remain as-is; (2) a separate fast-feedback validation track should be created; (3) recursive uplift can be usefully decomposed into execution, trust, and sequence layers; (4) a bounded administrative domain can serve as the first fast-feedback test.
>
> **Domain-specific lens:** Reviewing from the perspective of a social scientist who studies the relationship between administrative performance and public trust, and who is skeptical of the assumption that institutional competence produces trust on observable timelines.

---

### Top-line challenge: this exchange is converging on a comfortable answer that may not survive contact with evidence

Rounds 1 and 2 have produced something that looks like productive disagreement but is actually a negotiated settlement.

Round 1 says: the current memo is too slow to serve as an empirical test.

Round 2 says: agreed, but the memo is still valuable — just add a separate fast-feedback track.

That settlement is attractive. It lets the project keep what it has, add something new, and feel like it has responded to a genuine challenge.

But the settlement rests on an assumption that neither round has examined:

**that a fast-feedback track targeting bounded administrative domains can actually produce the kind of evidence that would matter for the recursive-uplift theory.**

I am going to argue that this assumption is much weaker than either round acknowledges, and that both rounds are treating it as solved because the alternative — that the theory may not be testable on any short timeline — is uncomfortable.

---

### 1. The convergence in this exchange is partly an artifact of shared framing

Per the adversarial protocol's own diagnostic: when contributors read the same prior context and respond sequentially, convergence is the expected outcome.

That is what happened here.

Round 1 introduced a frame: "separate the public memo from the fast-feedback test."

Round 2 adopted the frame immediately: "yes, separate them."

By the time Round 2 finished, the only remaining question was how to implement the separation — not whether the separation itself is the right move.

But the separation frame was accepted without testing whether the fast-feedback side of the split can deliver what the project needs.

Round 1 said the project needs "real evidence-based feedback." Round 2 agreed and proposed selection criteria. Neither round asked:

**What does existing evidence say about whether bounded administrative improvements actually produce measurable changes in trust or institutional legitimacy on short timelines?**

That is the question this round will address.

---

### 2. The administrative-burden literature does not support the assumption both rounds are making

Both rounds treat it as plausible that a well-chosen bounded domain — benefits access, records turnaround, permitting sub-processes — can produce short-cycle evidence on the execution-to-trust link.

Recent literature on administrative burden suggests this is harder than it looks.

The findings are consistent across multiple research streams:

- **Procedural efficiency does not reliably translate into public trust.** Improvements in processing speed, error reduction, or turnaround time are frequently experienced by the public as procedural rather than transformative. Citizens who interact with a faster benefits process do not automatically report higher trust in the institution that administers it.

- **Psychological costs dominate trust formation.** The administrative-burden literature increasingly distinguishes between compliance costs (time, paperwork), learning costs (navigating complexity), and psychological costs (stigma, stress, feeling undervalued). Trust erosion is disproportionately driven by psychological costs, which are harder to address through workflow optimization alone.

- **Technology-mediated improvements are ambivalent.** Digital tools often reduce compliance costs while introducing new learning costs. Citizens who interact with an automated system may experience faster service while simultaneously feeling less seen, less able to appeal, or less confident in the outcome. The net trust effect can be zero or negative even when the throughput metric improves.

- **The "speed of trust" problem.** Adoption of new administrative processes — by both staff and public — is constrained by pre-existing trust levels. An agency that is already distrusted does not get credit for a faster workflow. An agency that is already trusted gets credit whether or not the workflow is the cause. This makes it structurally difficult to attribute trust changes to bounded administrative improvements.

- **Single metrics are insufficient.** Scholars working on administrative burden increasingly argue that evaluating reforms requires mixed-method approaches: quantitative metrics on throughput and uptake combined with qualitative data on user experience, perceived fairness, and willingness to re-engage. That is not a 6-to-18-month measurement exercise. It is a research design problem that most bounded pilots do not attempt.

None of this means bounded administrative improvements are useless. It does mean that treating them as a reliable short-cycle evidence source for trust and legitimacy claims is not well-supported by the literature.

---

### 3. Round 2's three-layer decomposition may be analytically clean but empirically intractable

Round 2 proposed decomposing recursive uplift into:

1. **Execution layer:** can institutions deliver visibly better outcomes?
2. **Trust layer:** does visible performance improve trust, uptake, or willingness to engage?
3. **Sequence layer:** does trust make the next reform more feasible?

That is a reasonable analytical decomposition.

But it creates a problem the round did not address:

**Layer 1 is the only layer that is straightforwardly measurable on short timelines. And Layer 1 alone does not test recursive uplift.**

Layer 2 requires measuring trust, which — as the evidence above shows — is perception-based, confounded by pre-existing attitudes, and resistant to attribution from bounded interventions.

Layer 3 requires observing political and institutional sequencing effects, which both rounds already agreed are long-cycle phenomena.

So the decomposition clarifies the theory. But it also reveals that the part of the theory the project most wants to test — the trust-to-sequence link — is the part least amenable to short-cycle empirical methods.

Round 2 acknowledged this in passing ("It is much less likely to settle Layer 3 quickly") but treated it as an acceptable limitation rather than a structural problem.

I think it is closer to a structural problem.

If the fast-feedback track can only test Layer 1, and Layer 1 is not what makes recursive uplift distinctive, then the track risks producing results that are true but uninformative.

The project would learn: "yes, a bounded administrative domain can be made to work better."

It would not learn: "visible competence changes what becomes possible next."

That is the claim that matters.

### 4. The visible-competence-to-trust cascade has no direct empirical support

This is the hardest finding and the one the exchange should not avoid.

Neither round cited evidence for the core mechanism of recursive uplift: that visible institutional competence triggers a self-reinforcing cascade of trust, legitimacy, coalition durability, and expanded reform capacity.

Current public administration literature confirms that this mechanism is a plausible theoretical proposition, not an empirically validated phenomenon.

There is some evidence for institutional trust spillover — trust in one institution influencing trust in others — but the specific causal chain the project is proposing (visible competence → trust → coalition strength → reform feasibility → further competence) has not been isolated or confirmed in any study I can find.

That does not mean the theory is wrong.

It does mean both rounds are planning a fast-feedback track to test something that may not be testable the way they imagine.

The risk is:

- the project picks a bounded domain
- it measures execution improvements
- it surveys trust or uptake
- it finds ambiguous results
- it cannot tell whether the ambiguity is because (a) the theory is wrong, (b) the domain was too narrow, (c) the measurement was inadequate, or (d) the timeline was too short

That is not "real evidence-based feedback." That is a pilot that teaches the project less than it expected while consuming time and credibility.

### 5. The strongest absent perspective is someone who has run a bounded reform and watched trust not change

Both rounds reason from the project's internal analytical framework.

Neither round considers the perspective of a practitioner who has actually improved a bounded administrative process and then measured public response.

That practitioner would likely say something like:

> "We cut permit review time by 40%. Applicants noticed. Satisfaction scores went up slightly. But when we asked whether people trusted the department more, the answer was basically unchanged. When we asked whether the improvement made them more supportive of other reforms, they looked at us blankly. The improvement was real. The spillover was not."

That is not a hypothetical. It is a common finding in service-delivery evaluation literature. Bounded improvements are experienced as local fixes. They do not automatically reframe the citizen's relationship with the institution.

If the project builds a fast-feedback track expecting to see trust and sequence effects from a narrower domain, it may be designing for disappointment.

### 6. What would actually be adversarially useful here

I do not think the right conclusion is "abandon the fast-feedback track."

I think the right conclusion is:

**the exchange has been too quick to assume it knows what the fast-feedback track would test, and too slow to confront the possibility that recursive uplift's most distinctive claims may resist short-cycle validation entirely.**

That leads to three challenges the exchange has not yet resolved:

#### Challenge 1: the project may need to accept that some core claims are inherently long-cycle

Not every theory can be validated quickly. Recursive uplift, as currently formulated, makes claims about cascading institutional and political effects that unfold over years.

If the project insists on short-cycle evidence for those claims, it will either:

- test something narrower than the theory actually says (and learn less than it thinks)
- declare ambiguous results as partial validation (and weaken its epistemic discipline)

The more honest move may be to say plainly:

> the execution layer of the theory can be tested on short timelines; the trust and sequence layers cannot; the project will pursue both timescales and be explicit about which claims are supported at each stage

That is less dramatic than a fast-feedback track that promises to "test recursive uplift." But it is more truthful.

#### Challenge 2: the project may be under-investing in the kind of evidence it can actually get quickly

Round 2 identified three kinds of near-term feedback from the existing memo: reader-legibility feedback, practitioner-feasibility feedback, and theory-boundary feedback.

Those are genuinely fast.

If the project exposed the comparative memo to 10 serious practitioners — 5 in housing, 5 in AI governance — and asked "Does this framework name bottlenecks you recognize? ### Where I think Round 3 overstates its case

Round 3 is correct on the evidence. But it draws the boundary of "what the project can learn" too narrowly in two places.

#### 1. Round 3 treats trust as a single measurable quantity when it is actually a bundle of observable behaviors

The hardest version of Round 3's argument is: "you cannot reliably measure trust changes from bounded reforms on short timelines."

That is true if "trust" means the kind of thing captured by survey instruments — generalized confidence in an institution, willingness to say "I trust the government" on a Likert scale.

But the recursive-uplift theory does not actually require that kind of trust.

What it requires is closer to:

- **uptake:** do more eligible people use a service after it visibly improves?
- **re-engagement:** do people who have interacted with a reformed process come back rather than dropping out?
- **compliance:** do people cooperate more readily with adjacent requirements?
- **willingness to participate:** do people show up to public processes they previously avoided?

Those are not survey responses. They are behavioral indicators.

And recent public administration research does use behavioral proxies — uptake rates, re-engagement patterns, compliance shifts, participation in consultations — as indicators of institutional trust, precisely because they reflect actual behavior rather than stated attitude.

The methodological debate is real. Behavioral proxies can be confounded by factors other than trust: habit, coercion, self-interest, social norms. A direct causal link is hard to establish.

But they are still more observable on shorter timescales than generalized attitudinal trust.

So the project's fast-feedback track does not need to prove "trust increased" in a survey sense.

It may be able to observe: "after a visible improvement in X, behavioral indicators that correlate with trust moved in the predicted direction."

That is weaker than causal proof. But it is stronger than nothing. And it is honest about what it is.

Round 3 is right that the project should not promise trust validation from a bounded pilot. But it is too dismissive of behavioral proxies as a form of evidence that can at least begin to test whether the execution-to-trust link is real.

#### 2. Round 3 under-values the difference between workflow optimization and participatory reform

Round 3's practitioner vignette — "we cut permit review time by 40% and trust did not change" — is a fair representation of what happens when a bounded reform is purely operational.

But recent co-design and participatory design literature suggests that trust effects are significantly stronger when the reform process itself involves the people affected.

The key findings are:

- providing information about participatory processes improves perceptions of government benevolence and honesty, even among people who did not directly participate
- co-designed service improvements are perceived as higher quality and more acceptable than professionally designed ones, even when the substantive outputs are similar
- the trust effects of participation extend beyond direct participants to the broader public when the process is visible and well-communicated
- pure workflow optimization from an "insular" organizational culture is specifically associated with low public trust

This matters because it suggests that **how** the improvement happens may matter more for trust than **what** improves.

If the project selects a fast-feedback domain and treats it as a pure throughput exercise, Round 3 is right: trust will not move.

But if the project selects a domain where the reform process is itself participatory, visible, and co-designed with affected communities, the literature suggests the trust pathway is more plausible.

That is not a guarantee. It is a design criterion that the exchange has not yet named.

And it is one that connects directly to the project's own commitments: Principle 10 ("the future should be built in the open") and the framework's emphasis on visible competence rather than merely improved metrics.

So I would add a design requirement that neither prior round articulated clearly enough:

**the fast-feedback domain should not be selected only for its speed and measurability. It should be selected for whether its reform process can be participatory, visible, and co-designed — because those are the conditions under which the execution-to-trust link has the best existing evidential support.**

Those qualifications narrow the finding but do not reverse it.

**Confidence: Established by evidence, with qualifications from Round 4.**

### 6. Practitioner critique is the highest-value immediate fast feedback

Rounds 2, 3, and 4 converge on this. The project is upstream of intervention capacity. Structured expert review of the memo's analytical claims is faster, cheaper, and more directly informative than a bounded empirical pilot. Social science methodology supports structured expert critique as valid pre-empirical evidence for framework projects.

**Confidence: Working hypothesis, supported by methodological precedent.**

## What remains unresolved

### 1. Whether the fast-feedback track can deliver evidence that matters for recursive uplift

Round 3 argued it cannot. Round 4 argued it can under specific design conditions (participatory process + behavioral proxies + mixed methods). Neither side proved its case.

This is the most important open question. The project should treat it as a design challenge to be worked through, not as a settled matter.

### 2. Which specific domain should the fast-feedback track target

The exchange identified a search space (benefits access, records turnaround, permitting sub-processes, licensing, procurement) and revised the selection criteria. It did not select a domain. That is the right outcome — the criteria needed to be established before the search could be honest.

### 3. Whether behavioral trust proxies are informative enough for this theory

Round 4 introduced them. The methodological debate is real. Uptake, re-engagement, compliance, and participation correlate with trust but can be driven by other factors. The project should use them as directional indicators, not as proof.

### 4. How the project integrates evidence across tracks with different speeds and methods

Round 4 proposed an integration logic. It has not been tested. The project should revisit it when actual evidence arrives.

## Recommendations

These are ordered by priority and feasibility. The steward should be able to act on the first three within weeks.

### Recommendation 1: Ship `Memo 01` without delay

The comparative memo is the right public-facing artifact. Nothing in this exchange argues for replacing or delaying it.

The only revision this exchange requires: add a short epistemic-status section (or companion note) that names the evidential status of recursive uplift honestly.

Suggested language:

> Recursive uplift is this framework's most distinctive claim: that visible institutional competence in one domain can rebuild trust and create conditions for subsequent reform. This claim has structural support as the logical inverse of well-documented recursive degradation. But the specific causal chain has not been empirically validated. The execution layer is testable on short timelines. The trust and sequence layers are not — at least not with the kinds of evidence a bounded case can generate in months. The project will be explicit about which layers its evidence supports at each stage, and will report negative or ambiguous findings as honestly as positive ones.

### Recommendation 2: Launch structured practitioner critique within 4–6 weeks of publication

Design:

- 8–12 reviewers across housing, AI governance, public administration, and community organizing
- specifically recruit at least 2 practitioners who have run bounded administrative reforms and measured public response
- structured prompt asking: (a) does the framework name bottlenecks you recognize? (b) what does it miss? (c) where does the comparison help and where does it feel forced? (d) would you use or recommend this analysis?
- 4–6 week collection window
- synthesize findings in a short public note

This is the fastest, most informative learning available and it begins the adversarial protocol's own call for external human reviewers (Protocol §4).

### Recommendation 3: Formally separate the three kinds of "first" the project has been conflating

Add to the project's internal planning documents a clear distinction:

| Question                                            | Current answer                                                 | Status                                       |
| --------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| What is the first public orientation artifact?      | Comparative `Memo 01` (housing + AI)                           | Decided. Ship it.                            |
| What is the first fast-feedback learning mechanism? | Structured practitioner critique of `Memo 01`                  | Decided by this exchange. Begin immediately. |
| What is the first empirical validation case?        | To be designed. Criteria established. Domain not yet selected. | Open. See Recommendation 4.                  |

### Recommendation 4: Design (but do not rush) a fast-feedback validation case

The revised selection criteria, incorporating all four rounds:

1. Bounded institutional actor
2. Measurable public outputs within roughly a year
3. Plausible connection to **behavioral** trust indicators (uptake, re-engagement, compliance, participation) — not just throughput
4. A reform process that is itself **participatory, visible, and co-designed** with affected communities
5. Enough structural similarity to governance questions that the result teaches something about institutional capacity, not just process improvement
6. Enough public salience that the result matters beyond internal learning

Candidate search space (not yet evaluated against these criteria):

- benefits-access or redetermination systems
- public records or FOIA-style turnaround
- permitting sub-processes with short-cycle metrics
- licensing, inspection, or code-enforcement workflows
- procurement or grant-disbursement processes

The next exchange in this sequence should evaluate 2–3 candidates against these criteria and propose a specific case.

### Recommendation 5: Commit to transparent evidence integration in advance

Before any evidence arrives, the project should publish a brief note stating:

- what kinds of evidence each track produces
- that practitioner critique tests analytical usefulness, not causal claims
- that a fast-feedback case produces directional evidence on execution and behavioral trust proxies, not proof of recursive uplift
- that ambiguous results will be reported as ambiguous, with multiple interpretations presented
- that the trust-to-sequence layer (Layer 3) is inherently long-cycle and the project will not claim short-cycle evidence for it

This prevents the misuse risk Round 3 identified: ambiguous results being retroactively cited as partial validation.

### Recommendation 6: Treat this exchange's findings as revisions to the theory, not just to the evidence strategy

The most important output of this exchange is not the two-track architecture. It is the finding that recursive uplift, as currently formulated, bundles claims that operate on different timescales and under different evidentiary conditions.

The project should revise its internal description of recursive uplift to reflect the three-layer decomposition and the honest assessment of which layers are currently supported, which are testable, and which are not.

That revision should appear in the Systems Framework or in a dedicated theory-specification note.
