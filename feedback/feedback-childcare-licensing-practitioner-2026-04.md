## What the practitioner *did* say, decoded carefully

Three short sentences, each carrying signal:

### "After looking over this"

She opened the artifact. She tried. The friction was not at the access layer (the link worked, the page rendered). It was at the comprehension layer.

### "I have no clue what any of it says"

This is a strong negative comprehension signal from a person who is, in her professional life, navigating dense regulatory and operational material every day. Childcare licensing requires reading and understanding ordinances, state regulations, federal guidance, and inspection rubrics. This is not someone who finds technical text inherently inaccessible. The collapse here is specific to the project's text, not generic to written argument.

### "Too high definition to understand"

This phrase is the analytic centerpiece of the record. The practitioner did not reach for "too dense," "too long," "too jargon-y," or "too academic." She reached for a *resolution* metaphor. Three observations:

1. **Resolution implies fineness of detail relative to a viewer.** "High definition" suggests the artifact is *over-resolved* for the cognitive frame she could bring to it in the moment. Every sentence is doing too much load-bearing work; nothing is offered at lower resolution to orient from.
2. **The metaphor is gentle.** "High definition" is not pejorative; it implies the source material is fine, and the problem is the lack of a step-down path. This is consistent with her constructive posture.
3. **The metaphor implies a fix.** A high-definition image can be downsampled. The fix is not to lower the quality of the underlying analysis; it is to provide lower-resolution entry points that preserve the underlying signal.

### Why this follow-up is high-signal

1. **It is unsolicited.** The project did not run the Move 5 re-engagement protocol. Katelynn re-engaged on her own, after a gap of more than a week, with substantive operational content. This is evidence that the original "love to help where I can" register was not a courtesy reading; it was a real continuing willingness.
2. **It is operationally specific.** The April 28 message ("too high definition") was a comprehension complaint about the project's surface. This follow-up is _her own domain story_ rendered in five numbered issues plus a documented sign-permitting episode. The two together let the project triangulate: she could not parse the project's framing, but she can articulate her own experience clearly when the topic is the experience itself, not the project.
3. **It validates Round 4 Move 1's design intuition.** The Move 1 [Denver childcare-licensing flow](https://github.com/Civic-Blueprint/civicblueprint.org/blob/main/docs/CHILDCARE_LICENSING_FLOW_DENVER.md) was built on the bet that a domain artifact whose value does not depend on first accepting the project's framework would land where the framework itself did not. This follow-up — where Katelynn engages substantively the moment the topic is _her_ experience rather than the project's analysis — is consistent with that bet, while explicitly _not_ proving it (the artifact has not yet been shown to her).
4. **It surfaces structural friction patterns the Denver flow does not yet address.** Three of Katelynn's five issues, plus the entire sign-permitting episode, point at friction modes that are not in the current Denver flow:
   - **Scope-creep at inspection** — agencies require code items at inspection time that were not in the approved plans (Issue 1).
   - **Multi-track inconsistency** — informal pre-approval from one role (historic-district director) is overridden by formal review from another (permitting) (Issues 3 and the sign episode).
   - **Heritage / historic-district overlay** — a separate quasi-public review body whose meeting cadence (and quorum failures) becomes the rate-limiting step on construction (Issues 4 and 5).
     These are not Denver-specific in their structure. Whether to extend the Denver flow to surface them, or to start a sibling artifact for historic-district / sign-permitting overlay, is an Exchange #22 decision, not a feedback-record decision.
5. **It contains a second-order asymmetry claim.** Katelynn's email argues, with photographic evidence, that the city applies a code (brick-encased monument signs) to small businesses while not applying it to itself. Whether or not the specific code reading holds, the asymmetry pattern — different rules in practice for institutional vs. private actors — connects directly to [Principle 9](../PRINCIPLES.md#9-institutions-should-be-designed-for-competence-and-trust-not-theater) and to [Problem Map §13](../PROBLEM_MAP.md#13-institutional-distrust-is-becoming-a-governing-condition). It is one practitioner-grade datum behind a project-level claim.
