## What the practitioner *did* say, decoded carefully

Three short sentences, each carrying signal:

### "After looking over this"

She opened the artifact. She tried. The friction was not at the access layer (the link worked, the page rendered). It was at the comprehension layer.

### "I have no clue what any of it says"

This is a strong negative comprehension signal from a person who is, in her professional life, navigating dense regulatory and operational material every day. Childcare licensing requires reading and understanding ordinances, state regulations, federal guidance, and inspection rubrics. This is not someone who finds technical text inherently inaccessible. The collapse here is specific to the project's text, not generic to written argument.

### "Too high definition to understand"

This phrase is the analytic centerpiece of the record. The practitioner did not reach for "too dense," "too long," "too jargon-y," or "too academic." She reached for a *resolution* metaphor. Three observations:

1. **Resolution implies fineness of detail relative to a viewer.** "High definition" suggests the artifact is *over-resolved* for the cognitive frame she could bring to it in the moment. Every sentence is doing too much load-bearing work; nothing is offered at lower resolution to orient from.
2. **The metaphor is gentle.** "High definition" is not pejorative; it implies the source material is fine, and the problem is the lack of a step-down path. This is consistent with her constructive posture.
3. **The metaphor implies a fix.** A high-definition image can be downsampled. The fix is not to lower the quality of the underlying analysis; it is to provide lower-resolution entry points that preserve the underlying signal.
