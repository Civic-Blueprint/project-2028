# Reviewer Packet Template

> **Use case.** A steward-initiated invitation to a single external human reviewer to produce a scoped review of a specific project deliverable — most often a v2 deliverable from an adversarial-review exchange (e.g., [Exchange #21 Round 5](../agent/exchanges/government-overreach-ownership-ratchet-exchange.md#round-5) deliverables F1–F6) that needs perspectives sources cannot provide.
>
> **Companion document.** [REVIEWER_AS_A_ROUND_CONVENTION.md](REVIEWER_AS_A_ROUND_CONVENTION.md) specifies how the reviewer's response is integrated back into the exchange as a distinct round.
>
> **Derived from.** [Roadmap Thread F-packet (archived)](../ROADMAP_ARCHIVE.md#thread-f-packet--reviewer-packet--reviewer-as-a-round-convention-resolved) and [Roadmap Thread E (archived)](../ROADMAP_ARCHIVE.md#thread-e--sources-vs-live-humans-resolved-as-e1e2-scoping), which together argue that external review should be scoped narrowly to things sources cannot give — legitimacy, dialogic negation, unknown-unknowns, tacit practitioner knowledge — and should not attempt to replicate the analytical breadth the agents can already produce.

---

## Design principles for this packet

1. **One reviewer, one deliverable, one week.** The packet is sized for one person to produce useful review on one specific artifact within a one-week response window. Recruiting *one* live reviewer is the target; the project does not need a panel to derive value from this format.
2. **Scoped to what sources cannot provide.** The packet explicitly does not ask the reviewer to generate more sources, to disagree with the literature, or to cover ground the project has already covered analytically. It asks for (a) *legitimacy review* and/or (b) *practitioner feasibility* and/or (c) *perspective gaps the reviewer can name that the project cannot*.
3. **Orientation before ask.** A reviewer who has never seen the project must be able to understand what the project is, why the review is being asked, and what "done" looks like from a single short read — *before* engaging the specific deliverable.
4. **Respect for time.** The packet names the maximum total time the review is expected to consume (target: 2–4 hours including reading, writing, and one optional follow-up exchange). A reviewer who cannot spend that time should be able to decline clearly with no loss of goodwill.
5. **Minimum-prerequisites presentation.** The packet carries enough context inline that the reviewer does not *need* to read other project documents to engage the specific deliverable, though it links to them for reviewers who want more context.
6. **Explicit provenance.** The packet is honest about the fact that the project is developed through steward-curated human-AI collaboration, that the deliverable being reviewed was produced by that process, and that the reviewer's contribution will be attributed as a distinct, human-authored round.

## Template

Below is the full template. Steward fills in the bracketed fields per-invitation. All headers and their ordering are part of the convention and should be preserved across invitations so reviewers who review more than one deliverable over time encounter a consistent structure.

---

```
Subject: Civic Blueprint — invited review of [deliverable title] ([~2–4 hour ask])

Hi [Name],

Thanks for agreeing to look at this. Here is the packet.

This is a [one-week | two-week] ask. If at any point it becomes a larger
time commitment than that, please say so and we will scope it down rather
than have you carry it past your capacity.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
PART 1 — Orientation (≈ 5 minutes)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

What Civic Blueprint is.
Civic Blueprint is an open, collaborative project analyzing why critical
systems fail and what it would take to redesign them without producing
new pathologies. It is not a manifesto. It is a working blueprint.

How the documents are produced.
The project's analytical documents are produced through iterative drafting
by multiple AI models operating under structured review protocols, then
synthesized, edited, and approved by the project steward (me). The
steward is accountable for what is published. Review protocols exist to
surface the failure modes of that process — convergence bias, shared
framing, confident nonsense — rather than hide them.

What this packet is for.
This is an invitation to produce a single, scoped human review of one
specific deliverable. Your review will be integrated into the exchange
that produced the deliverable as a distinct round under your name (if
you consent to attribution; see PART 5 below).

What this packet is not.
- Not a request to endorse the project.
- Not a request to rewrite the deliverable or to produce new analysis
  comparable in scope to what the project has already done.
- Not a request to read the full body of project documents. You do not
  need to, and the packet is designed so you do not have to.

What is being asked of you specifically.
[One sentence stating the specific form of review requested. Pick one of:
  (a) Legitimacy review — does the framing honestly represent the
      tradition/practice/community you are in a position to speak for?
  (b) Practitioner feasibility review — would a proposal built on
      this deliverable actually work in the operational context you
      know from practice?
  (c) Perspective-gap review — what is this deliverable missing that
      you, from your specific standpoint, can see?
  (d) Some combination of the above, explicitly named.]

Why you.
[Two or three sentences naming the specific standpoint, practice, or
tradition the steward is hoping you will speak from. Be specific — not
"your expertise" but "your experience as [role] in [context] over
[time period]." Be clear that you are not being asked to represent an
entire tradition or community, only to speak from your own standpoint
within it.]


──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
PART 2 — The specific deliverable (≈ 20–30 minutes)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Title: [Deliverable title, e.g., "Principle 5 v2 — inclusive
institutions with bounded rules"]

Derivation: [One-line derivation, e.g., "Exchange #21 Round 5
Deliverable 1 (v2), responding to adversarial Round 4 challenges."]

Provenance label: [human / collaborative / ai-generated, steward-curated
/ ai-generated, per docs/CONTENT_PROVENANCE.md]

The deliverable in full:
[Paste the deliverable verbatim. Do not summarize. If the deliverable
is a commitment statement, include it, the rationale table, and any
attached companion notes. Include the section numbering used in the
source document so the reviewer can cite specific clauses.]

What the deliverable is responding to:
[Three to five sentences of context. Name the adversarial challenges
from the prior round that the deliverable is trying to respond to.
Name the prior version of the deliverable, if there was one, so the
reviewer can see what has changed.]

What the deliverable does *not* cover, by design:
[Two to four bullets naming things the reviewer might otherwise expect
to see addressed but that are handled elsewhere. This prevents the
reviewer from critiquing the deliverable for something it is not
attempting to do.]


──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
PART 3 — Focused questions (≈ 60–90 minutes)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Please answer as many of these as you can, in any order. Free-form
prose is welcome; bulleted notes are welcome; "I don't have a view on
this" is welcome.

The questions below are tuned to the review type specified in PART 1.
Steward picks ONE of the three sets (or combines when multiple types
were requested):

── For LEGITIMACY review ──
1. Does the deliverable accurately represent the tradition, community,
   or practice you are in a position to speak from? If not, where does
   it misrepresent?
2. Are there claims in the deliverable that would feel extractive,
   exoticizing, or appropriative if this document were circulated in
   your community of practice? If so, name them specifically.
3. Are there absences — things your community would expect to see in a
   document making these commitments — that are not present? What?
4. If this deliverable were adopted and invoked in proposals targeting
   your community, what safeguards would your community want that are
   not currently in the deliverable?

── For PRACTITIONER FEASIBILITY review ──
1. In the operational context you know from practice, would a proposal
   built on this deliverable work? If not, where does it break?
2. What "obvious" things does this deliverable miss that anyone inside
   your context would catch immediately?
3. Are the five (or N) authenticity conditions / requirements / checks
   it specifies the right ones, in the wrong order, or missing any?
4. If you had to name one thing the deliverable gets right and one
   thing it gets wrong from a practitioner standpoint, what are they?

── For PERSPECTIVE-GAP review ──
1. Reading the deliverable, where do you see a perspective or angle
   that is absent — specifically, absent in a way the project appears
   not to have noticed?
2. What does the deliverable implicitly assume about the world that
   would not hold from where you sit?
3. Which of the claims in the deliverable would feel different — or
   be contested — if they were framed from the standpoint you bring?
4. Is there a neighboring deliverable the project has not yet produced
   that, from your standpoint, would be more important than this one?


──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
PART 4 — How your review will be used (≈ 5 minutes)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Your review will be integrated into the exchange that produced this
deliverable as a distinct round — Round [N+1] — titled "External
review from [Name or pseudonym, per your choice]."

The agent stewards of the exchange will then produce one more round
in response, treating your review as the primary input. That response
round will either:
  (a) revise the deliverable to integrate your findings, producing a
      v3 version;
  (b) flag the disagreement as a substantive open question in the
      project's research debt and update the Roadmap accordingly; or
  (c) both, if some findings prompt revision and others prompt open
      questions.

You are not being asked to review the response round. If you want to,
that is welcome, but it is not part of the ask.

The steward will send you, after the response round is complete,
a short note describing how your review was used, what changed in the
deliverable as a result, and what remains open. This is project
practice, not optional.


──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
PART 5 — Attribution, provenance, and consent (≈ 2 minutes)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Attribution choice. You may choose how your review is attributed:
  (a) By name, with affiliation of your choice (or none).
  (b) By a pseudonym you specify.
  (c) Anonymously, attributed as "external reviewer with background
      in [broad category, e.g., 'municipal housing practice']."

Content you retain the right to review.
Before the review is published (that is, before the exchange document
it goes into is merged to the main branch of the project repository),
the steward will send you the final text of your round as it will
appear, including any light editorial changes (typography, formatting,
linking to other project documents). You have the right to request
changes, withdraw the review, or change your attribution choice. This
offer remains open until publication; after publication, the review is
treated as a citable part of the project record.

Consent to AI response.
You consent that after your review is integrated, the project's
agent-driven process will produce a response round, and that response
round is not written or approved by you. The project is committed to
not putting words in your mouth; the agent response is clearly
attributed as agent-authored steward-curated content and is not
presented as your position.

Data handling.
The review is published in the public project repository. The steward
does not retain private notes, correspondence, or drafts beyond what
is needed to produce the exchange round. If you want the correspondence
itself kept private, say so and the steward will honor that.


──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
PART 6 — What I want the reviewer to know, honestly
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This project is developed at a fast pace by a single steward working
with AI agents. It is easy to mistake that for a project that has
more resources, more people, or more authority behind it than it
does. It does not. The steward's ability to do this work depends on
people like you being willing to contribute real review rather than
polite engagement.

If your honest reaction is "this deliverable is wrong in ways the
project has not caught," that is the contribution that is most
valuable. Politeness hurts the project. The project's review protocols
are designed specifically to surface that kind of finding; your review
round is the external instance of that design.

If your honest reaction is "I do not have a useful view on this
specific deliverable but I could offer one on [a different one]," say
so. The steward will take that seriously.

If your honest reaction is "I cannot do this in the time available,"
decline. No follow-up needed.

Thank you for reading this far.

— [Steward name]
```

## How to tailor the packet per invitation

1. **Pick the review type(s).** One of (a) legitimacy, (b) practitioner feasibility, (c) perspective gap — or an explicit combination. In PART 3, include only the question sets that match the chosen types.
2. **Name the standpoint specifically.** The "Why you" paragraph in PART 1 should be concrete. "You have worked as a municipal planner in a jurisdiction that used CEQA in practice for fifteen years" is usable; "your planning expertise" is not.
3. **Paste the deliverable verbatim.** Do not summarize. If the deliverable is long, consider splitting into two invitations rather than truncating.
4. **Name what the deliverable is *not* doing.** The "What the deliverable does *not* cover, by design" subsection prevents false-positive critique.
5. **Match the time estimate to the deliverable.** The defaults are 2–4 hours total. If the deliverable is shorter or the review type is tighter (e.g., legitimacy-only on a specific clause), cut to 1–2 hours. If it is longer, split into two invitations. Do not exceed a 4-hour total-time ask on a single invitation without explicit prior negotiation with the reviewer.
6. **Use provenance labels honestly.** The deliverable's provenance label (per [CONTENT_PROVENANCE.md](CONTENT_PROVENANCE.md)) should be in the packet. If the deliverable is `ai-generated, steward-curated`, say so. Do not present it as `collaborative` to make it seem more human-authored than it is.
7. **Send the final response-round note.** After the agents' response round is complete, send the reviewer the short summary described in PART 4. This is a project-level commitment, not a polite gesture.

## How this packet fits into the project's review infrastructure

- The [Adversarial Review Protocol](../agent/process/adversarial-review-protocol.md) runs inside the agent-steward exchanges.
- The [Coherence Audit Protocol](../agent/process/coherence-audit-protocol.md) checks internal consistency.
- The [Historical Parallel Test Protocol](../agent/process/historical-parallel-test-protocol.md) grounds proposals against historical cases.
- **This packet + the [Reviewer-as-a-Round Convention](REVIEWER_AS_A_ROUND_CONVENTION.md) are the external-human-reviewer instrument.** They exist because — per [Roadmap Thread E (archived)](../ROADMAP_ARCHIVE.md#thread-e--sources-vs-live-humans-resolved-as-e1e2-scoping) — the agent process is strong at source-mediated review and weak at dialogic negation, unknown-unknowns, tacit practice, and legitimacy. This instrument targets those specific gaps.
