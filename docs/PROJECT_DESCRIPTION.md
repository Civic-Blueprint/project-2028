## The framing in one idea

Civic Blueprint has **two layers**, and the most accurate description names both:

- **The *what* — a blueprint.** A diagnosis of why core systems (housing, AI, capital, governance) stay broken and who benefits, plus principles and concrete reform proposals.
- **The *how* — engineering-grade method.** The part that's genuinely novel: policy ideas are treated like testable hypotheses, adversarially reviewed (including by independent AI models blind to each other), version-controlled, and revised in the open when they fail.

The "tools and machinery for governance, like SWE has for software" instinct is **accurate for the *how* layer** — it's the sharpest, most distinctive part. Just pair it with the *what* so it doesn't sound like neutral tooling, and steer clear of the "govtech" misread (see [What to avoid](#what-to-avoid-or-adjust)).

## Lines by length

**One-liner (substance + method):**

> "It's an open-source attempt to design a more capable, humane society for the AI age — and to build it with the same rigor engineering uses for reliable systems, instead of the way political vision usually gets written."

**~20 seconds:**

> "Most big-picture policy gets produced like a manifesto — someone writes their view and there's no real mechanism to catch where it's wrong. We're doing the opposite: every claim is a testable hypothesis, everything gets adversarially reviewed and red-teamed, and it's all version-controlled and revised in the open. It's less 'here's my plan for the world' and more 'here's a blueprint that has to survive being attacked — plus the process for attacking it.'"

**~60 seconds (academic / technical):**

> "Think of it as two layers. The *what* is a blueprint: a diagnosis of why core systems — housing, AI, capital, governance — stay broken and who benefits, then principles and concrete reform proposals. The *how* is the part I think is genuinely new: we treat policy design like a research program. Claims are written to be falsifiable, they're put through structured adversarial review — including independent AI models that don't see each other's work — and we keep version history so you can see what failed and why. The goal isn't to *assert* a vision; it's to build one that *earns* confidence by surviving challenge. It's early-stage and built in the open, and honestly the thing I most need is sharp people willing to try to break it."

## Framing by audience

Same method, two dialects — pick the one that fits the person:

- **Engineers:** "SWE discipline for policy" — version control, code review, CI, reversible decisions ("two-way doors").
- **Academics / researchers:** "the scientific method and peer review, applied to societal design" — falsifiable hypotheses, red-teaming, replication across independent reviewers.

## What to avoid or adjust

- **The "govtech" misread.** "Tools and machinery for governance" can sound like *software for government agencies* (portals, digital services). Clarify: we're building the **design discipline** for producing and stress-testing policy — not products for bureaucrats.
- **The "value-neutral tooling" undersell.** Unlike a linter, the project takes real normative positions (the Principles). It's a blueprint *with* machinery to keep it honest — not just machinery.
- **Don't overclaim maturity.** It's early-stage, built in the open, explicitly trying to be proven wrong. That honesty makes serious people take it *more* seriously, not less.

## The bridge to the ask

The honest "why I'm reaching out" follows naturally from the method: the project explicitly needs **external domain experts and human adversarial reviewers** — people willing to try to break the strategic claims. "Above my pay grade" technical or academic contacts are exactly the red team the project is looking for.
